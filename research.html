    RESEARCH

    Analyzing the Impact of Person-Centered Dementia Care

    OVERVIEW

    Alzheimer’s disease is the only top-10 cause of death in the United States with no known cure or effective treatment options. Alzheimer’s and related dementias are progressive disease that ultimately results in the affected person needing assistance with most everyday activities. As such, Alzheimer’s disease is the most expensive disease in the nation. Total annual payments for health care, long-term care and hospice care for people with Alzheimer’s or other dementias are projected to increase from $305 billion in 2020 to more than $1.1 trillion in 2050.

    It is estimated that over 90% of people with Alzheimer’s and related dementia experience troubling symptoms, including aggression, anxiety, and sadness, which can also have a profound physical and emotional impact on informal care partners and formal caregivers.


    RESEARCH EFFORTS

    Preliminary research shows that personalized behavioral interventions, such as reminiscence therapy and socialization, can improve clinical outcomes and reduce costs for people living with dementia and their caregivers. In my role as head of product at Generation Connect, I have led collaboration with researchers at the University of Wisconsin to assess how technology can empower care teams to provide person-centered care in an effort to improve clinical outcomes and reduce costs.


    OUTCOMES
    • Co-authored a study published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research on how tablets can improve moods for older adults with dementia and interactions with their formal caregivers.
      • The study was one of the first in the U.S. to assess how simple interactions with tablets could benefit those with dementia and their caregivers.
      • Out of 1,089 care recipients and caregiver engagement sessions, our results revealed promising potential, demonstrating people with dementia positive mood changes resulted from using music, YouTube as the sole app, and multiple engagement strategies.
      • Pilot group data indicated the tablet engagement improved client mood outcomes, reduced caregiver turnover, and extended client length of stay in home.
      • Sessions that resulted in positive mood changes for people with dementia also positively influenced the caregivers’ day.
    • Preliminary research motivated further exploration of systems for collecting data and potential cost savings for healthcare providers.
    • Applied for a 2020 National Institute of Health (NIH) Small Business Innovation grant in partnership with researchers from the University of Wisconsin.
